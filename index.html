Culture of Japan

Japan is known for its rich cultural traditions and vibrant festivals. Here are some of the most famous:

  • Gion Matsuri – Kyoto's month-long festival held in July with parades and traditional costumes.
  • Awa Odori – A famous dance festival in Tokushima held in August.
  • Sapporo Snow Festival – Held in Hokkaido in February with massive snow and ice sculptures.
  • Tanabata – Celebrated in July or August with wishes tied to bamboo trees.
  • Obon – A Buddhist event in August honoring ancestors with dances and lanterns.

🛬 Arrival & Legal Setup in Japan


When you arrive in Japan on a mid- or long-term visa (like work, study, or family), there are a few important steps you need to complete to legally live and work here.


📇 Residence Card

When you land at the airport, immigration will issue you a Residence Card (在留カード) if you’re staying more than 90 days.

  • What it is: Proves your visa and legal status.
  • Why it matters: Required for contracts, banking, and daily life.
  • Tip: Legally required to carry it at all times.

🔖 Work Permission (if applicable)

Students or dependents need 資格外活動許可 (Permission to Engage in Other Activities) to work part-time.

  • How to get it: Request it at the airport immigration counter upon arrival.
  • Why it matters: Without it, part-time work is illegal.

🏠 City Registration

Within 14 days of moving in, visit your local City/Ward Office (区役所 / 市役所) to register your address.

  • Why: Links your address to your Residence Card.
  • Bring: Residence Card and passport.
  • Tip: Some offices have English help desks.

🩺 Health Insurance

All residents must enroll in National Health Insurance (国民健康保険).

  • Where: Same City/Ward Office during address registration.
  • Why: Reduces medical cost to ~30%.
  • Card: Mailed to your registered address.

🆔 My Number

After registering, you’ll receive a postcard to apply for a My Number Card (個人番号カード).

  • What: National ID number for taxes and services.
  • Tip: Apply online or in person with the QR code.

  • Use approved, color-coded city bags.
  • Sold at convenience stores and supermarkets.

  • Burnable: 2–3 times/week
  • Non-burnable: 1–2 times/month
  • Recyclables: Weekly (can vary by type)
  • Oversized: Appointment only

Missed the day? Store it until next time.


🪑 Oversized Garbage

  • Register online or by phone.
  • Buy a sticker (処理券), place it on the item.
  • Leave item at designated location on pickup day.

⚠️ Common Mistakes

  • ❌ Wrong bags → not collected
  • ❌ Mixed trash types → rejected
  • ❌ Wrong collection day → fines or warnings

🆘 Community Support & Safety

Disaster Preparedness in Japan

Japan is prone to natural disasters such as earthquakes, typhoons, and tsunamis. It's important for all residents to be prepared and informed.

  • Recommended Apps: Yurekuru Call (earthquake alerts), NHK News (official news), Safety Tips (multi-language disaster info).
  • Emergency Kit: Include water, food, flashlight, battery-powered radio, medications, first-aid items, passport, and cash.
  • Know Your Area: Locate your nearest evacuation shelters and check the hazard map from your local city hall.
  • Monthly Drills: Some communities and schools conduct disaster drills — participation is encouraged.

Smoking Rules in Public

Japan has strict laws regarding public smoking to ensure public health and cleanliness.

  • Designated Areas: Only smoke in clearly marked smoking zones — these are usually near train stations or public parks.
  • No Street Smoking: Walking while smoking is prohibited in many cities such as Tokyo and Kyoto.
  • Penalties: Fines for smoking in non-smoking zones can range from ¥2,000–¥5,000.
  • Vape Rules: E-cigarettes and vapes follow the same restrictions as regular cigarettes.

Community Etiquette

Being part of a Japanese community means respecting shared spaces, participating in activities, and maintaining harmony.

  • Greetings: A simple "Konnichiwa" (hello) to your neighbors can foster good relationships.
  • Noise Awareness: Keep noise levels down, especially after 9 PM. Thin walls in apartments are common.
  • Join Local Events: Many communities host festivals (matsuri), clean-up days, and seasonal gatherings. It's a great way to meet people.
  • Garbage Sorting: Proper trash separation and collection day awareness shows respect for your community.

🎉 Community Involvement

Living in Japan means joining the community and respecting shared spaces.

📢 Quietness & Neighbor Relations

  • Japanese apartments often have thin walls, so:
    • Avoid loud music or speaking at night (10 PM – 7 AM).
    • Do not slam doors or run in hallways.
  • A simple greeting like “Konnichiwa” builds goodwill with neighbors.

🧹 Community Cleanups & Festivals

  • Many neighborhoods organize clean-up events (ゴミ拾い or 街の掃除).
  • You may be invited to join local festivals (祭り / Matsuri).
  • It’s a good opportunity to:
    • Meet local residents
    • Learn culture and language
    • Show you're part of the community
